Happiness Scores of Countries

The World Happiness Report is an annually released report ranking the happiness of countries based on several quality-of-life indicators of the people of each nation. It was first introduced in 2012 and has been released annually since 2013.The report outlines happiness of each country and the causes of happiness and misery. This helps the policy making bodies in countries to create their policies to address their citizens needs and improve their happiness.

Bhutan is a country that Gross National Happiness instead of Gross Domestic Product as their main development indicator. In 2011, the General Assembly of United Nations adopted Resolution 65/309 sponsored by Bhutan that invited nations to give more importance to happiness and well being in determining how to achieve and measure social and economic development.

In 2012, the United Nations' General Assembly resolution 66/281 proclaimed 20th of MArch as International Happiness Day. It recognized the relevance of happiness and well-being and the importance of their recognition in public policy objectives.

There are six factors considered when determining happiness scores of country:

  1. GDP per Capita
  2. Social Support
  3. Healthy Life Expectancy
  4. Freedom to Make Choices
  5. Generosity
  6. Perception of Corruption

Based on the results of the World Happiness reports from 2015 to 2023 the happiest region in the World is Western Europe and Norht America and ANZ. Finland from the Western Europe region is the happiest nation in the world. The Sub-Saharan African region is the unhappiest region in the world. However Afghanistan in the South Asian region is the unhappiest country in the world.

The Six Factors that determine the Happiness Scores

1. Gross Domestic Product per Capita

Gross Domestic Product per Capita is the Gross Domestic Product, which is the total value of all goods and services produced within a country's borders in a specific period, usually a year or a quarter divided by the population. Based on the data provided by the World Happiness reports countries with higher happiness has higher GDP per capita. High GDP per capita generally indicates a higher standard of living and more economic opportunities for the residents of a country, while low GDP per capita can suggest lower living standards and potentially higher poverty levels.

2. Social Support and 3. Freedom to Make Choices

Social Support measures whether if you are in need is there sufficient support and Freedom to Make Choices measures whether a citizen is satisfied with their freedom to make life choices. When analysing the data we can see that countries with higher social support index and higher freedom to make choices index have a higher happiness scores. For example, Iceland has a social support score of 1.46, freedom to make choices score of 0.66 and happiness score of 7.52, while Afghanistan has a social support score of 0.27, freedom to make choices score of 0.07 and happiness score of 2.99.

4. Healthy Life Expectancy

Healthy life expectancy values are based on the data extracted from the World Health Organization's (WHO) Global Health Observatory data repository. We can see after 2020 there has been a drop in the healthy life expectancies values in all regions, especially in the North America and ANZ region. This could be a result of the COVID19 pandemic and the US having the highest number of COVID related deaths. But generally, we can see that regions with higher healthy life expectancy values haviing higher happiness scores.

5. Perception of Corruption

Perception of Corruption values are measures the perception the public has whether there is widespread corruption in the government and also whether there is widespread corruption in businesses. It is interesting to see that regions with higher happiness scores such as Western Europe and North America and ANZ region have higher perception of corruption values compared to other regions in the world. This means the citizens of this countries have less trust in there government and businesses.

6. Generosity

Generosity values are a measurement of whether the citizens of a country have donated to chairty or not. Based on the findings, the happiest regions in the world doesn't necessarily mean that they are the most generous. For an example, North America and ANZ region has an average happiness score of 7.2 and a generosity value of 0.34, while Africa has an average happiness score of 5.1 and a generosity value of 0.51.
